After 2022, every newsroom in Europe needed a way to talk about gas flows that wasn't either a Reuters wire or a Stratfor PDF. Editors started asking for "a map of Nord Stream" and getting back static infographics dated three months prior.

The Atlas is the third option: open, source-cited, status-aware. Hover any line; see who built it, who shut it, what year, what for. Geopolitics read through what's actually on the ground — not through what the press release said.

01 / Brief

No one had a live map.

Trigger
Energy crisis
Europe · 2022–24

Sources existed — IEA, EIA, Global Energy Monitor — but as PDFs, spreadsheets, or paywalled dashboards. Almost nothing was a visualisation you could give a junior reporter and have them produce a usable map in twenty minutes.

The brief I gave myself: cover every major piece of oil and gas infrastructure that matters geopolitically in 2026, on a clickable map, with status (active / damaged / shut / proposed) as a first-class concept. No login. No paywall. No API key.

02 / Approach

Status as a first-class data field.

Data model
WGS84 coordinates
Status enum
Year built
Operator

Every pipeline carries: route waypoints, capacity, length, year built, operating company, current status, and a short detail line ("Sabotaged Sep 2022. Three of four lines ruptured.").

The status enum drives both colour and dashing: solid = active, dashed = limited, faded = shut, dotted = proposed, broken = damaged. This means a single glance at the European map tells you what's running, what's paused, and what's just on paper.

Status changes are versioned. When Drujba shut down in Dec 2024, it became a one-commit update — not a redesign.

03 / Outcome

Seven maps, one model.

The world map is the entry point; each continent has its own dedicated detail page with regional context (e.g. the Europe map foregrounds Ukraine transit, Druzhba, Nord Stream; Asia foregrounds Power of Siberia, Middle East chokepoints).

Coverage at ship: 90+ pipelines, 80+ extraction fields, 70+ LNG terminals, 12 strategic chokepoints. Mobile layout fixed mid-2026 to use a collapsible info panel — on phones, the map is the priority, not the legend.

04 / Stack

Tools.

Tech
Leaflet.js v1.9
CartoDB Dark tiles
Pure JS

Leaflet for the map layer. CartoDB Dark for the tiles — a flat, neutral basemap that doesn't compete with the data. All data lives in JSON files alongside the HTML, served as static files from GitHub Pages.

No backend. No build step. Cost of running: zero. Time to fork and host your own copy: under five minutes.
